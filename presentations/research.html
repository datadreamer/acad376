Discerning & Making

Research Techniques

Aaron Siegel | | Fall 2017

Discovering Your Subject Matter.


Prompt.


How were you introduced to the subject? What are your initial thoughts? What is your initial perspective?

Inspiration.


What are your emotional motivations? What makes you passionate?

Interest.


What do you find intellectually compelling about the subject?

Asking the right questions.


Increase Understanding.


The more you know, the better your questions get.

Hypothesis.


What do you think is going to happen? What would happen if __________?

Methodology.


How do you intend to proceed?

Understanding the Processes Involved.


Stardust.


Trace the processes your subject has undergone going back to its cosmic origins.

Steps as Variables.


Changing a step in a process can affect the results of steps that follow as well as the end result.

System Design.


Reduce or add steps in the process. Optimize steps to be faster or more productive. Streamline operation of system components.

Information Acquisition.

Knowledge Platforms.


Wikipedia.


Easy to search and read. Quick summaries of ideas and topics. Links to relevant subjects. References to additional knowledge sources.

CIA World Factbook.


Definitive information about different places. Statistics on a wide variety of topics.

Wolfram Alpha.


Natural language search for information. Calculates results of queries from stored data.

Libraries.


Books.


Look it up. Visit a library. Learn the dewey decimal system. RTFM. Flip through them on the spot or check them out for extended review.

Digital Collections.


Photos, videos, maps, legal documents.

Archives.


Newspapers, trade journals, academic dissertations, whitepapers.

Blogs.


Perspective.


Different viewpoints, backgrounds, opinions, reader demographics, intentions.

Process.


Linear recording of events.

Expertise.


Straight from the source on a specific subject.

Forums.


Conversation History.


Discussion between multiple people on a specific topic. Fixed in time. May be over or continuing.

Thought Process.


Each post reveals more in a process. Discussion, debate, troubleshooting, debugging, tutoring.

Mixed Perspectives.


Different people, different levels and areas of expertise, different methods of contributing to the thread.

Databases.


Data Dumps.


Downloadable packs of archived data. Large and old, but full of good content.

APIs.


Live access to a system hosting data. Query system for type of data you want. Tiny to medium results, most recent data.

FOIL Request.


No access to public data exists, so submit a freedom of information law request for a specific data set. Very slow but potentially incredibly rewarding with lots of vabluable data. NYC Taxi Viz. NYC Taxi Drop Offs. Privacy issues with taxi data.

Information Management.

Storage.


Google Drive.


Create collaborative documents. Upload files and media. Desktop application syncs content to your hard drive.

Dropbox.


Syncs folder content between hard drives on participating computers. Share any kind of files or media.

Github.


Uses version management repositories to enable shared development of projects. Developed for code but useful for any text-based revisions.

Organization.


Social Bookmarks.


Tagging, group bookmarking, comments. Only stores a reference to sites or media. Research Chronology.

Pinterest.


Organize visual material. Keep reference of visual research, moodboards, inspirational content.

Wiki.


Construct your own collaborative space for knowledge management. Construct pages, link subjects together, format content, edit continuously and improve. All revisions are stored.

Collection Habits.


Process Book.


Keep track of your progress and your findings. Post regularly; maintain a schedule to motivate you to keep posting. Post publicly; hold your progress accountable to others.

Packrat vs. Collector.


Collect everything, including some junk, or collect only things that fit your criteria and leave out other things you come across.

Research Goals.


Set goals for the amount of research you want to do. Number of articles, images, videos, books to acquire each day, week, etc.

Information Analysis.

Synopsis.


1. Highlight.


Read through your selected material and highlight the important items in the text.

2. Trim.


Take only the highlighted elements and isolate them from the rest of the body text.

3. Reconstruct.


Create a new sentence or short paragraph combining all of the elements from the highlighted material to create a concise summary.

Morphological Analysis.


1. List components.


Create a detailed list of the components that contribute to your subject.

2. Create grid.


Place the list on the vertical and horizontal axis' of the grid.

3. Compare components.


Make X/Y comparisons to see what interactions each component has with every other component.

Statistics.


Keep a Tally.


What elements of your research are quantifiable? What metrics can be applied to record measurements?

Averages & Deviations.


What is the average value or condition in your subject? How much variation is there in results? What's the standard deviation from the mean?

Correlation != Causation.


Two factors may appear to be related, but that doesn't necessarily mean that one is the cause of the other.

Data Visualization.


Charts & Graphs.


Histograms, Line Graphs, Scatter plots, Pie Charts. Basic one or two dimensional representations of quantitative information.

Visual Language.


Pair data variables with visual variables in order to create a consistent language for interpretation. More variables, more dimensions, more complexity.

Interface to Data.


Interacting with data allows viewers to explore it and pose their own questions.
